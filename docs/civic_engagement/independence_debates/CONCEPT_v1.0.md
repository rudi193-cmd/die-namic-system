## The Hook

**What if presidential debates were anchored in the Constitution itself, not cable news talking points?**

Two former presidents. Four weeks of debates. Building to midnight on July 4th, 2026, in the Oval Office with two American citizens. Everyone holds a pocket Constitution. Jon Stewart asks if any of it was real. Jimmy Fallon makes them sing the national anthem.

**Tagline:** "The most American debate ever: equal parts spectacle and substance, ending at midnight on July 4th with the question—was any of it real?"

---

## The Problem

Traditional debates are broken:
- Moderators ask "gotcha" questions
- Candidates deliver pre-written soundbites
- Nobody learns anything new
- Voters are exhausted

But also:
- We need *some* way to evaluate leaders
- Democracy requires informed choice
- Debates still draw massive audiences
- The format just needs reinvention

**The Question:** What if debates revealed *how* candidates think, not just *what* they promise?

---

## The Solution

Replace moderator questions with:
- Constitutional passages (the actual text)
- Historical photographs (civic decisions from the past)
- Citizen stories (real Americans, real stakes)

The format forces:
- Engagement with actual documents (can't dodge the text on screen)
- Long-form thinking (3 rounds per topic, 5 minutes each)
- Genuine negotiation (Week 1's hot mic)
- Listening to citizens (Week 4's 4-hour finale)

**It's harder to bullshit a photograph than a moderator's question.**

---

## The Structure (4 Weeks)

### Week 1: The Negotiation & First Debate (~60 minutes)

**Part 1 — Hot Mic Negotiation (7 minutes)**
- Can they agree on one Constitutional passage together?
- Clock visible, counting down
- Everything audible

**Part 2 — Preparation Break (30 minutes)**
- They separate, consult advisors, prepare arguments
- Cameras follow both (split screen)
- Obama studying vs. Trump preparing = fascinating contrast

**Part 3 — The Debate (20 minutes)**
- They return and debate the passage they chose together
- 3 rounds:
  - Round 1: "What does this passage mean?"
  - Round 2: "How should we interpret it today?"
  - Round 3: "How would you apply it as president?"

**Tests:** Can they negotiate? How do they prepare differently? Can they interpret the same text they agreed on?

---

### Week 2: The Organizations (~2.5 hours)

- 13 national organizations each pick a Constitutional passage
- Labor, business, faith, environment, civil rights, veterans, etc.
- Candidates respond to each organization's chosen passage
- Organizations represent diverse American constituencies

---

### Week 3: The Candidates (~90 minutes)

- Each candidate picks one Constitutional passage
- 30-minute notice exchange (neither knows what the other chose until just before)
- Reveals personal values through what they consider most important
- Forces genuine interpretation, not rehearsed talking points

---

### Week 4: July 4th Finale (4 hours + 6-hour break)

**Block 1 — Lincoln Memorial (2:00 PM - 4:00 PM)**
- Public spectacle setting
- Citizen 1 shares their story
- Both candidates respond
- Fireworks, crowds, patriotic atmosphere

**Break (4:00 PM - 10:00 PM)**
- 6-hour break, cameras follow both
- Trump: rallies, supporters, media appearances
- Obama: study, reflection, family
- Contrast reveals character
- America watches how they each spend July 4th

**Block 2 — Oval Office (10:00 PM - Midnight)**
- Intimate setting, just six chairs
- Citizen 2 shares their story
- Deep conversation, no audience
- Building toward midnight

**The Convergence (11:50 PM - Midnight)**
- 11:50 PM: Jon Stewart enters, asks hard questions
- 11:56 PM: Jimmy Fallon surprises Stewart with guitar
- Stewart doesn't know Fallon is coming
- Midnight: All six sing the national anthem together
- Fade to black
