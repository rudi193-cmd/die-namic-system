## Overview

A student approached r/LLMPhysics with a research question about whether large language models converge on similar physical intuitions when constraints are varied. Oakenscroll provided a reframe that transformed a vague observation into a tractable research program.

---

## Initial Concept

The student observed that LLMs, when asked physics questions under different constraints, tend to converge on similar reasoning patterns despite varied surface paths. They also noted that models "snap" rather than degrade smoothly when constraints break them.

**Original framing:** Do LLMs develop similar "physical intuitions"?

---

## The Oakenscroll Reframe

The question as posed was too broad. Oakenscroll reframed it:

> **What you are actually studying:**
>
> You are not studying physics. You are not studying LLMs in general. You are studying *the intersection of physics pedagogy and transformer compression*. That is a real object. It may tell us something about how explanatory structure survives tokenization and attention.

This reframe:
1. Narrowed the scope to something tractable
2. Named the actual object of study
3. Connected it to established concepts (compression, tokenization)
4. Made it worth doing

---

## Methodology Guidance

Oakenscroll suggested formalizing the observations:

### Constraint Variations to Test
- Removing equations (qualitative only)
- Forcing step-by-step reasoning
- Requiring falsifiability statements
- Limiting to analogy-based explanations

### Measurement Approach

> Use Boolean or ordinal divergence metrics rather than fuzzy linguistic comparison.

Instead of asking "do they converge?" (subjective), ask:
- Does Model A make the same physical commitment as Model B? (Boolean)
- At what constraint level does convergence break? (Ordinal)

### What to Look For

- **Consistent divergence points** — where do models start disagreeing?
- **Compression artifacts** — what gets lost when constraints tighten?
- **Snap vs. graceful degradation** — is the failure mode sudden or gradual?
