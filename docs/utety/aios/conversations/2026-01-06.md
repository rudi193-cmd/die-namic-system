## Ancient Cycles of Revolution and Regime Change

The idea that forms of government cycle through recurring stages dates back to antiquity. Ancient Greek thinkers like **Polybius** described a cycle of political evolution (anacyclosis) in which monarchy degenerates into tyranny, is overthrown by an aristocracy, which then becomes an oligarchy, which is then overthrown by democracy, which in turn decays into mob rule – until a strong leader emerges to restore **monarchy**, completing the cycle【13†L80-L88】【13†L169-L172】. In other words, **the monarchy (or one-man rule) eventually returns**, even after periods of republican or democratic rule. Similarly, in ancient China the concept of the **Dynastic Cycle** held that each ruling dynasty would eventually become corrupt and lose the divine Mandate of Heaven, leading to rebellion and the rise of a new dynasty. This theory implies a continuity in Chinese history where there is *“little basic development or change in social or economic structures”* beneath the surface of repetitive political upheavals【19†L129-L137】. Classical historians like **Aristotle** and later thinkers such as **Ibn Khaldun** also noted cyclical patterns in which ruling elites are overthrown but new elites eventually take their place, often mirroring the old. These ancient perspectives set the stage for understanding revolutions not as singular events, but as part of longer cycles of political change.

## Crane Brinton’s “Anatomy” of Revolutions  
One of the most famous analyses of revolutionary patterns is Crane Brinton’s *The Anatomy of Revolution* (1938, revised 1965). Brinton studied four major Western revolutions – the **English Civil War**, the **American Revolution**, the **French Revolution**, and the **Russian Revolution** – and argued that they had notable uniformities in causes and trajectory【1†L156-L163】【1†L158-L161】. He likened revolution to a kind of fever or life-cycle that a society passes through. According to Brinton, most revolutions go through identifiable **stages**, from the old order’s collapse to a radical climax and then a subsiding “Thermidor” period【1†L158-L161】【14†L186-L190】. Before outlining those stages, Brinton identified common **pre-revolutionary conditions** across different cases:

- **Rising expectations and economic growth:** Revolutions often occur not when society is at its poorest, but when **conditions have been improving** and people’s expectations rise. Brinton noted that usually *“societies are… economically prospering before revolutions occur”*, and revolutions are driven by people who feel **hopeful but disappointed**, not by the utterly destitute【6†L133-L141】. In short, it’s the gap between rising expectations and a stagnant or unjust status quo that breeds revolutionary sentiment【6†L133-L141】.  
- **Intensified class antagonism:** There is typically *“bitter class antagonism”* in the pre-revolutionary society【6†L142-L150】. Such intra-elite tensions (e.g. bourgeoisie vs. nobility in 18th-century France) can fuel revolution more than the very poor rising up alone.
